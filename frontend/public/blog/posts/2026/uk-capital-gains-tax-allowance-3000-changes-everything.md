The UK tax landscape for investors has undergone a seismic shift as we navigate the 2025/2026 tax year. The most glaring change for stock market investors and property owners alike is the **drastic reduction of the Annual Exempt Amount for Capital Gains Tax (CGT) down to just £3,000**. 

If you've been casually trading stocks on platforms like Interactive Brokers, Freetrade, or Trading 212, this new threshold fundamentally alters how you need to approach your investment accounting.

### The incredible shrinking allowance

Just a few years ago, the CGT tax-free allowance sat comfortably above £12,000. It provided a massive buffer for retail investors, allowing them to take substantial profits—or rebalance their portfolios—without having to worry about HMRC or filing complex self-assessment returns. 

Today, with the allowance slashed to £3,000, thousands of everyday investors are suddenly finding themselves in the tax net.

### Higher Rates for Gains

Not only has the allowance dropped, but the rates you pay on those gains have increased. As updated in the recent budget, the main rates of CGT (for assets other than residential property) are now:
*   **18% for Basic-rate taxpayers**
*   **24% for Higher-rate taxpayers**

This marks a significant increase that directly eats into your investment compounding if your investments are held in unwrapped, general investment accounts (GIAs).

### What you must do in 2026

1.  **Embrace Tax Wrappers First**: The absolute easiest way to bypass these harsh new rules is to maximize your £20,000 Stocks and Shares ISA allowance every single year. Gains inside an ISA are completely invisible to HMRC and do not count toward your £3,000 limit.
2.  **Calculate Meticulously**: With only a £3,000 buffer, winging your tax math is dangerous. Utilizing precise tools, like our UK Capital Gains Tax Calculator for IBKR and other brokers, ensures you know exactly how much of your allowance you've eaten up, applying the strict 30-day "bed and breakfasting" rules and section 104 pooling correctly.
3.  **Prepare for HMRC Reporting**: Even if you think you've stayed under the £3,000 gain limit, you might still legally be required to file a return. If your total proceeds (the total value of all shares you sold in the year) exceed four times the annual allowance (£12,000), HMRC mandates that you report your trades.

The golden age of ignoring Capital Gains Tax as a retail investor is over. With the £3,000 allowance, active portfolio management and pristine record-keeping are your best defenses against unexpected tax bills.
