With the Capital Gains Tax (CGT) allowance slashed to just £3,000 for the 2025/2026 tax year, a massive wave of retail investors are calculating their net returns, breathing a sigh of relief when they realize they only made £2,500 in profit, and assuming their tax obligations are zero.

However, HMRC possesses a lesser-known reporting requirement that acts as a trap for highly active traders: **The 4x Proceeds Rule.**

### What is the 4x Proceeds Rule?

Even if your total capital gains for the year fall below the £3,000 tax-free allowance, HMRC still requires you to report your trades on a Self Assessment tax return if the **total proceeds from your asset disposals exceed four times the annual exemption.**

For the 2025/2026 tax year, four times the £3,000 allowance equates to **£12,000**.

### How Active Traders Get Caught

If you are an active trader, day trader, or swing trader, generating £12,000 in total sales proceeds is remarkably easy. 

For example:
1. You purchase £5,000 worth of AAPL and sell it a week later for £5,200 (Gain: £200, Proceeds: £5,200).
2. You purchase £7,000 worth of TSLA and sell it a month later for £6,900 (Loss: £100, Proceeds: £6,900).

Your net *gain* for the year is only £100. This is well below the £3,000 allowance, meaning you owe **£0 in Capital Gains Tax.**

However, your total *proceeds* from disposals are £5,200 + £6,900 = **£12,100**.

Because £12,100 is greater than the £12,000 threshold, **you are legally obligated to file a Self Assessment tax return declaring these trades.**

### The Burden of Proof

HMRC enforces this rule to ensure that investors aren't obfuscating massive trading volumes that could potentially harbor miscalculated underlying taxable gains. 

When you do file, you cannot simply write "Gains were under £3,000." You must provide a highly accurate summary of your total proceeds, your allowable costs, and your net gains or losses for the year. This requires pristine record-keeping, factoring in complex regulations like Section 104 pooling and 30-day bed and breakfasting rules.
