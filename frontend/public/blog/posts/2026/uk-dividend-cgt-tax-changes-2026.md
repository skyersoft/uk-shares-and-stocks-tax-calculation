With the UK set to implement several key changes to Capital Gains and Dividend Taxes in the 2026 tax year, investors and business owners need to prepare for shifting financial landscapes. The adjustments taking effect from April 2026 aim to modify the tax implications for investment income, impacting how you should structure your portfolios and extract profits from your limited companies.

Here is a comprehensive breakdown of what you need to know:

### 1. Dividend Tax Rates Are Increasing

If you earn income through dividends, whether from a general investment account or by paying yourself as a company director, you will soon owe more to HMRC. 

Starting in April 2026, the tax rates on dividend income will increase by a flat 2 percentage points for both basic and higher rate taxpayers:
- **Basic Rate:** Increasing from 8.75% to 10.75%
- **Higher Rate:** Increasing from 33.75% to 35.75%

The additional rate of dividend tax, however, will remain unchanged at 39.35%. 

Importantly, the tax-free dividend allowance will also remain frozen at £500 for the 2026/27 tax year. As dividend taxation becomes less shielded and rates climb, extracting profits from limited companies via dividends is becoming less tax-efficient. Directors should review their salary and dividend splits and consider options like increasing employer pension contributions to optimize tax outcomes.

### 2. Capital Gains Tax (CGT) Update

For the tax year starting April 6, 2026, the baseline CGT environment remains somewhat stable, but there is a notable change for business owners claiming relief.

- **Annual Exemption:** The tax-free allowance for individuals will stay at £3,000. While low compared to historical levels, at least it isn't dropping further.
- **General Rates:** Standard capital gains rates will remain at 18% for basic rate taxpayers and 24% for higher and additional rate taxpayers.

**Crucial Change to Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR):**
A significant hike applies to gains where Business Asset Disposal Relief (formerly Entrepreneurs' Relief) or Investors' Relief is claimed. The tax rate for these disposals will rise from 14% to 18% for transactions made on or after April 6, 2026. The lifetime limit for qualifying gains will remain at £1 million.

If you are a business owner contemplating a sale, the timeline of your disposal could significantly impact your final take-home amount due to this rate hike. 

### 3. Frozen Thresholds Lead to Fiscal Drag

In addition to the explicit rate increases, the wider tax environment continues to subtly increase tax burdens. The personal allowance and income tax thresholds will remain frozen until at least April 2031. 

Due to inflation and rising wages, this freeze naturally drags more individuals into higher tax brackets over time—a phenomenon known as "fiscal drag." As incomes grow, a larger proportion of your earnings, and subsequently your investment returns like dividends and capital gains, could fall into higher taxation bands.

### Prepare Your Strategy Now

The 2026 changes to dividend and capital gains taxes underscore the importance of utilizing tax-advantaged accounts. Maximizing your £20,000 ISA allowance and contributing to pensions will be more crucial than ever to shelter your investments from rising tax rates. 

If you regularly harvest gains, receive substantial dividends, or plan to sell a business asset soon, consult with a financial advisor or tax professional to adapt your strategies ahead of the April 2026 changes.
