Over the next 12 months, the UK tax system is going to experience its biggest overhaul in a decade, and many taxpayers are still unaware of how significantly it will affect them. 

From the rollout of "Making Tax Digital" (MTD) to incoming hikes on dividend taxes and changes to property tax rules, there is a lot to prepare for. Here is a breakdown of the key changes starting in 2026 and what you need to do now to stay ahead.

### Making Tax Digital (MTD) is Finally Here

The single biggest change coming on April 6th, 2026, is Making Tax Digital. If you qualify, the days of submitting a single annual self-assessment tax return in January are over. Instead, you will be required to submit quarterly summaries of your income and expenses to HMRC—four times a year instead of just once.

**Who does it affect?**
For the 2026/27 tax year, MTD applies to sole traders, partnerships, and landlords with a total taxable turnover of £50,000 or more. Note that this threshold is based on *turnover*, not profit, and it only applies to self-employed and rental income (excluding PAYE salary, pensions, or dividends). 

The rollout timeline continues:
- **From April 6, 2026:** Those with over £50,000 turnover
- **From April 6, 2027:** Those with over £30,000 turnover
- **From April 6, 2028:** Those with over £20,000 turnover

**What do you need to do?**
1. **Use Approved Software:** Spreadsheets alone are no longer enough. You must use HMRC-approved software (like FreeAgent, Xero, QuickBooks, or Sage) to link digitally to HMRC.
2. **Track Your Turnover:** Ensure you know exactly what your total income is before expenses.
3. **Separate Finances:** If you haven't already, open a separate business bank account for self-employment or rental income. This is essential for MTD compliance and tracking your turnover accurately.

### Dividend Tax Increases for Company Directors

If you pay yourself through dividends from a limited company, you will soon pay more tax on the exact same profit. 

While the tax-free dividend allowance remains at a minimal £500, the tax rates on everything above that are increasing:
- **Basic Rate:** Rising from 8.75% to 10.75%
- **Higher Rate:** Rising from 33.75% to 35.75%

For a director extracting £50,000 in dividends, this translates to an extra £1,000 in tax every single year. As the tax efficiency of dividends shrinks, limited company directors must carefully review their salary versus dividend split to optimize their tax bill. For instance, in some scenarios, increasing your salary could provide benefits like reduced corporation tax and higher pension contributions that offset the increased income tax and National Insurance.

### The Triple Hit for Landlords

Landlords are facing a perfect storm of changes:
1. **Higher Tax Rates:** From April 2027, tax rates on rental income are increasing by two percentage points across all bands (basic rate will rise to 22%, higher rate to 42%, and additional rate to 47%). For a landlord with £30,000 in rental profits, that's roughly £600 more in tax per year.
2. **Quarterly Reporting:** MTD means HMRC will now see your figures throughout the year, with zero flexibility for end-of-year catchups.
3. **Renters' Rights Act:** This act introduces the abolition of "Section 21" no-fault evictions, stronger tenant protections, and tighter compliance rules.

Furthermore, the loss of certain allowances, like the wear and tear allowance for furnished properties, means landlords must now track actual maintenance and repair costs rather than claiming a flat rate.

### Prepare Now, Don't Panic Later

The biggest risk isn't that these changes exist—it's failing to adjust your financial structure in response to them. If you’re a landlord, a sole trader, or a limited company director, now is the time to review your setup. Ensure you are familiar with accounting software well before the July 2026 deadline for your first quarterly MTD submission, and consult with an accountant to optimize your tax position before these rules take full effect.
